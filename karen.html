From Dr Kelly Page

So why would we be interested in how open you are?

Growing out of the premise that our socially relevant and salient persona is encoded in language[1] early researchers started to explore human habits or our disposition towards certain behaviours, thinking or emotion to explain differences. This became known as trait theory[2]. The use of trait theory in profiling practices is relative to long held view of traits (and modern day testing of them) as differing across individuals (or groups of individuals), relatively stable over time and as influences of what we do [2][3].

Today five personality traits: openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism (called the Five Factor Model) dominate much of our consciousness and our computer screens when we consider tests of personality. However early researchers on trait theory in the 1920's and 1930's [2][3][4] considered over 4,000 adjectives to explain human traits, which were reduced to 171 in the 1950's[4][5][6] , with different multiple item test developed to measure sixteen, five and then three traits of personality respectively in the 1960's[7][8][9]. In 1961Tupes and Chrystal examined data collected from eight different samples, ranging from airmen with no more than high-school education to first-year graduate students, and included ratings by peers, supervisors, teachers, or experienced clinicians in settings as diverse as military training courses and sorority houses[8]. This work provided further weight to a five-factor structure of personality traits. However, it was not really until the 1980's [10][11][12] that there was widespread acceptance among personality researchers of five traits that "held the most promise" for our understanding of differences (or similarities) in human personas.

Openness to experience is one of these domains (which has also been labelled inquiring intellect or, intellectual interests). Treated as a continuimn people high on openness are considered as being motivated to seek new experience, being curious and imaginative and divergent thinkers compared to people who may test low on openness[13][14]. Low scorers are considered more comfortable with familiar and more traditional experiences, and may be conservative and resistant to change. Researchers have also found as openness increases so too does perceptions of subjective wellbeing [15], intelligence [16] and acceptance and use of social media [17][18], and higher rates of use of marijuana [19] and the drug ecstasy[20].

From Dr Kelly Page

Neuroticism, another domain of the Five Factor Model of personality, is characterized by a large number of different emotional responses to the world around you. Emotional responses such as anxiety, fear, moodiness, worry, envy, frustration, jealousy, and loneliness are all typical indicators of one’s emotional stability [1] - or neuroticism. Treated as a continumn, people who score high on neuroticism are more than average likely to feel a large number of emotional responses such as those mentioned above. While research has purported that neuroticism is a potential risk factor for some more common mental condition such as depression and panic disorders [2], moderate levels of neuroticism may provide benefits, such as increased drive and productivity, due to greater sensitivity to the world around[3].

Your emotional stability can also be related to how your response to potentially confrontational situations. For example, Dave became quite confronting of you during the experience. How you responded to this could be influenced by how you test on neuroticism, as neuroticism is considered positively associated with distancing/avoidance behaviours[5].

From Dr Kelly Page

Why was Karen interested in how much control you felt over your life? Locus of control since the 1950’s has become an aspect of personality studies in health, education and workplace psychology. It refers to the general expectancy about the extent to which reinforcements are under internal or external control. That is a person’s internal belief that reinforcements are determined largely by personal effort, ability and initiative or by other people, social structures, luck and fate[1][2][3].

Our feelings of control over our life and the experiences we have, like your experience with Karen, can have a pretty profound impact on other areas of your life and your sense of wellbeing. Studies of locus of control report that people with an internal locus of control often engage in activities that will improve their situation, work hard to develop their knowledge, skills and abilities, are inquisitive and more likely male [5][6][7]. They are also more likely to choose innovative and non-traditional work roles[4][5][7] and compared to people who self-report external locus of control, internals have better overall physical health, mental health and quality of life [9].

In contrast due to their locating control outside themselves, externals tend to feel they have less control over their fate, tend to be more stressed and prone to clinical depression [9][10], less job satisfaction than internals [4][6][7] and earn less money [11][12]. In one study of over 7,500 British adults (followed from birth), who had shown an internal locus of control at age 10, were less likely to be overweight at age 30 [13].

FROM DR KELLY PAGE

So why did we ask you this question? To answer this we thought we'd provide you with a bit of history about life coaching. The origin of the term 'coach' is derived from the Hungarian word, Kocsi meaning 'carriage' or a medium of transport [1]. Interestingly this term was first used as slang in reference to an instructor or private tutor term at Oxford University in the 1830's to describe someone who 'carried a student through an exam.'[1] It is since then that the practice of coaching has developed to be an informal and personal experience of helping to carry a person from where they are to where they wish to be in their life, through the offering of advice and guidance.

Today, coaching is often considered as a useful tool for individual and organizational development[2] and is a very popular practice used in leadership, management and self-development circles. In fact, life coaching is purported to be a 2 billion dollar industry[3] and in use by senior executives to sports professionals to it being offered as a health benefit for employees in some workplaces.

Unlike traditional psychotherapy, where the therapist is an expert and healer who diagnoses the patient's pathology, a life coach - like Karen - is instead part partner, motivator and confidante, and sometimes a critical friend. The International Coach Federation (ICF) defines coaching as "partnering with clients in a thought-provoking and creative process that inspires them to maximize their personal and professional potential."[4] Someone who helps you to think about meaningful goals and helps to set action to move forward toward those goals and also to explore your life and interaction with others. By downloading Karen you became not just her client, or a user of the app, you became a partner in the co-creation of life coaching experience.

Now as coaching is often a private one-to-one experience, we wanted Karen to also be intimate and personal for you. This was in part why we chose an app on your phone so she is always with you, and in part why we chose film based storytelling as a way to develop your relationship with Karen who at first is to you a complete stranger. This is somewhat akin to how today many us increasingly use Facetime, Skype and Google Hangouts.

This question centrally focuses on which life goal is most important to you right now, in the moment you completed the question. Usually people seek out and engage with a life coach for a reason. They have something they want to work on to make better or some area of their life they feel they need guidance on. In a 2003 exploratory study about life coaching - one of the first empirical studies to explore its impact, the authors purported that life coaching has promise as an effective approach to personal development and goal attainment in nonclinical populations[5]. The authors also defined life coaching as a result-oriented, systematic process in which the coach facilitates the enhancement of life experience and goal-attainment in the personal and/or professional lives of normal, non-clinical clients'(p.254) [5].

To provide this similar context for Karen we asked you to select what area of your life you might want to work on with Karen. Not only did this provide Karen with information to begin profiling you it also gave you more of a sense of ownership and an intrinsic motivation to continue the experience as you made the decision Karen didn't make it for you[6][7][8].

So what could your selection communicate about you to others?

There is an entire field of enquiry about material cultures and why things matter in our lives[1]. It is an interdisciplinary field drawing from and informing philosophy, anthropology, linguistics, socio-psychological and many others and all with the purpose of telling of relationships between people and things. Daniel Miller‘s "Material Cultures: Why Some Things Matter" and the series “Semiotics, Communication, Cognition”, published by De Gruyter Mouton provide a deeper synthesis of its evolution and current day applications.

This field has been a matter of interest for decades for anthropologists and archaeologists alike who infer meaning from objects as they trace and piece together the historical associations of things across communities and cultures. They use objects as meaning-making, narrating past lives[2] through the analysis of how objects may be both active and passive in meaning making.

From Dr Kelly Page

So why is this so important? Or is it?

Privacy has been discussed and debated from many different perspectives. One’s right to be let alone is a very early definition of privacy that stems from an 1890’s article titled the ‘Right to Privacy.’[1] In short it refers to our individual right to be immune from scrutiny or being observed in our private settings, such as our own home. This corresponds with a notion of control over our personal information about ourselves that others have access to[2], as well as the role this information plays in our social relationships.

To develop personal, intimate relations with others we have to disclose information about ourselves and we might even invite people into our homes and our personal networks, just as Karen did. As such intimacy is inextricably linked to privacy.[1]. In fact how we share about others is critically important for our social relationships and the health of our social networks. Our respect for others privacy can and does impact emotionally and socially not only our friends and family members but also a complete stranger - like Karen and our relationship with them.[3]

With the rise in our use of social and mobile media, from mobile apps to social networks, these systems are designed to facilitate our ubiquitous content sharing and sociability. With their use comes a responsibility in how we share about others, from family members such as young children to strangers like Karen[4]. Mobile phones especially are a rich source of personal information about individuals. They are also very personal devices that act as an extension of our personal selves.

In a 2012 study conducted on mobile phone users in the USA, an overwhelmingly large number consider information stored on their mobile phones to be private — at least as private as information stored on their home computers[7]. They also overwhelmingly reject several types of data collection and use drawn from current business practices. This responsibility was once mainly the domain of people of certain professions such as journalists who underwent training to determine the extent of legitimate public interest in the information. In this a journalist was expected to distinguish between a right to knowledge and a curious interest in knowing[5].

This has given rise to what some are referring to as the ‘privacy paradox’, a situation where people freely provide personal data (about themselves and others) while displaying difference in their intentions to disclose personal information[6]. During your interaction with Dave we asked you three questions from which we could judge the degree to which you respected Karen’s right to her personal privacy. While Karen did invite you into her home and her life, she didn’t invite you into her room (nor did Dave into his).

As opposed to use existing items, we developed these items uniquely for the Karen experience so that they were personal and reflective of the context within which they were tested.
